# The Ladder — Deck Abstract

*Synopsis of the lecture deck "How computers and data work — a climb from switches to AI."*

---

**One field, two ladders climbed at once.**

The deck teaches how computers and data work, from electrical impulses up to generative and agentic AI. Its organizing insight is that this single climb is really two parallel ladders ascended in the same motion — and that you cannot fairly judge what today's AI can and cannot do without feeling both.

**Ladder A — structural / ontological (electricity → meaning).** Information becomes "real" by climbing layers. At the bottom: transistors as fast switches, and bits that mean nothing until a convention reads them. The processor pushes symbols with trivial, deterministic operations at enormous speed; memory forces the first physical tradeoff — near-and-fast versus far-and-large — so transforming, then storing, is always a compromise; the operating system rations finite resources so that many programs appear to run at once. Then instruction becomes human-shaped: code is machine steps written in words, translated exactly down to the metal; an algorithm is a recipe whose scaling decides what is feasible. Then composition becomes the discipline of building: decompose hard tasks and seal each behind a "what" that hides its "how"; in practice you glue borrowed parts and every abstraction eventually leaks; so you build defensively, anticipating that networks drop, code drifts, and inputs lie. This whole half is exact, inspectable, reproducible — meaning is composed upward from mechanism, with no magic.

**The hinge.** A step-back interlude names the ideas that recur at every rung: understand one layer below, everything is a tradeoff, the map is never the territory — and then, pointing forward, the second ladder itself: from here up, reach is bought with certainty.

**Ladder B — epistemic (certainty → probability).** The learning and AI halves spend the guarantees the classical stack kept, in exchange for reach. The paradigm inverts: stop writing rules, show examples, and let the machine find the rule — the data becomes the program. Objects become points in a space where similarity is geometry; but the patterns found are correlation, not cause, and the world drifts away from the training snapshot; learning means generalizing to the unseen, gradable only against a proxy, with irreducible error; so a prediction is usable only with an honest measure of its own confidence — certainty, once lost, can at least be measured.

The AI paradigm is this epistemic layer at full scale. Generative language models pursue one deflationary objective — predict the next word — from which grammar, facts, and something like reasoning emerge as side effects; scale is the engine, billions of parameters tuned by colossal parallel arithmetic, with new abilities appearing "as if from nowhere." And the economics split into a strong asymmetry: training is parallel and heavy — a massive one-time cost you can spread across many processors at once — while inference is sequential and light, one token after the last, so serving it shifts the pressure from raw compute to latency and throughput. This makes AI the non-deterministic top layer of the abstraction stack: instead of composing elementary operations strictly, you specify a context and let a statistical translator produce a result — more reach, weaker guarantee, the two ladders meeting at their floor. The closing rung — agentic AI — is not new theory but an instantiation: give a probably-right system tools and a goal, and its guesses become actions that compound. Every earlier idea (drift, leaky abstractions, the spec that is not the need, understand-one-layer-below) reappears with consequences, and the human verifying step stops being optional. The least-certain layer we have built is being handed the most capacity to act — which is precisely why the whole ladder is worth understanding.

**The through-line:** each rung is more powerful than the last, and — higher up — less certain. The lecturer on stage is the verifying step the top layer can no longer promise for itself.
